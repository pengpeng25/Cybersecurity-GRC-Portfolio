# GDPR Legal Framework

> A structured reference guide covering the core principles, data subject rights, and organizational obligations under the EU General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation EU 2016/679).

---

## 1. What Is GDPR

The **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)** is a data privacy law enacted by the European Union in **May 2018**. It establishes a unified legal framework for the protection of personal data across all EU member states.

### Core Objectives (Article 1)

| # | Objective |
|---|-----------|
| 1 | Lay down rules for the protection of natural persons with regard to the processing of personal data |
| 2 | Protect fundamental rights and freedoms, in particular the right to protection of personal data |
| 3 | Ensure the free movement of personal data within the Union is neither restricted nor prohibited |

### Who Does GDPR Apply To?

**In scope:**
- Organizations operating within the EU and its member states
- Citizens and residents of the EU
- Organizations outside the EU that process personal data of EU individuals
- Organizations with 250+ employees processing EU data
- Web retailers targeting EU users (e.g., European languages, Euro pricing)
- Organizations using web tools that track EU user data

**Out of scope:**
- Personal or household activity (e.g., collecting email addresses for a private event)
- Organizations outside the EU with fewer than 250 employees may be exempt from some record-keeping obligations

> **Key principle:** GDPR is not about the data controller or its location — it is about the **data subject**. If EU citizens or residents interact with your service, GDPR likely applies.

## 2. Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Personal Data** | Any information relating to an identified or identifiable individual — name, address, government ID, email, IP address, geographic location, pseudonym |
| **Special Categories of Personal Data** | Data revealing race, ethnicity, sexual orientation, political/religious/philosophical beliefs, genetic or biometric data, health information, union membership |
| **Data Subject** | Any individual whose personal data is collected and whose privacy rights are protected under GDPR |
| **Data Controller** | Any organization that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data (e.g., retailers, service providers, employers) |
| **Data Processor** | A natural or legal person engaged by the controller to process, store, transmit, or destroy personal data on their behalf |
| **Data Protection Authority (DPA)** | An independent national public authority responsible for enforcing GDPR in each EU member state |
| **Data Protection Officer (DPO)** | A GDPR expert within an organization responsible for ensuring compliance; required in certain cases |

## 3. The Six GDPR Principles (Article 5)

These principles form the foundation of all GDPR compliance obligations. Failure to comply risks penalties of up to **€20 million or 2–4% of global annual turnover**, whichever is higher.

| # | Principle | Requirement |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **Lawfulness, Fairness & Transparency** | Process personal data according to the law, fairly, and with full transparency to the data subject |
| 2 | **Purpose Limitation** | Collect data only for explicitly stated and legitimate purposes; no further processing beyond those purposes (exceptions: public interest archiving, scientific/historical research, statistics) |
| 3 | **Data Minimization** | Collect only data that is adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary |
| 4 | **Accuracy** | Take every reasonable step to ensure personal data is accurate and kept up to date; inaccurate data must be erased or rectified without delay |
| 5 | **Storage Limitation** | Retain identifiable personal data only as long as necessary for the stated purpose, then securely destroy or archive (same exceptions as purpose limitation) |
| 6 | **Integrity & Confidentiality** | Implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect against unauthorized processing, accidental loss, destruction, or damage |

## 4. Data Subject Rights

GDPR grants individuals **eight fundamental privacy rights**. These rights give data subjects meaningful control over how their personal data is collected, processed, and shared.

### 4.1 Right to Be Informed (Articles 13–14)

Data subjects must receive explicit details **before** data collection begins:

- Controller's identity and contact details
- Purpose of data processing
- Legal basis for processing
- Third-party recipients
- Data retention period
- Rights granted under GDPR
- Right to file a complaint with a DPA
- Whether data provision is a statutory or contractual requirement

> Information must be communicated in **clear and plain language**, typically via a privacy notice.

### 4.2 Right of Access (Article 15)

- Data subjects can access and view their personal data at any time
- They can request **free copies** of their data
- Controllers must maintain accessible channels for these requests

### 4.3 Right to Rectification (Article 16)

- Data subjects can request correction of inaccurate data
- Controllers must rectify within **one month** of the request
- Best practice: allow subjects to update their own data via profile settings

### 4.4 Right to Object to Processing (Article 21)

- Data subjects can object to processing even after giving consent
- A **lawful basis** is required for the objection
- If the controller wishes to deny the request, they must demonstrate a lawful basis for continued processing

### 4.5 Right Not to Be Subject to Automated Decision-Making (Article 22)

- Protects against decisions made solely by automated systems (e.g., automated loan rejection = potential profiling)
- Subjects can decline automated decision-making at consent stage or request human intervention later

**Exceptions:**
- Automated processing required to enter into or complete a contract
- Explicit consent from the data subject
- Authorization by EU or member state law with sufficient safeguards

### 4.6 Right to Erasure / Right to Be Forgotten (Article 17)

- Data subjects can request permanent deletion of their personal data
- Controllers must also delete copies held by third parties

**Exceptions:**
- Freedom of expression and information
- Legal obligation compliance
- Establishment, exercise, or defense of legal claims
- Public interest, scientific/historical research, or statistical purposes

### 4.7 Right to Data Portability (Article 20)

- Subjects can request their data be transmitted to another controller
- Data must be in a **structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format**
- Applies only to data the subject has directly provided

### 4.8 Right to Restrict Processing (Article 18)

Processing must be restricted when:
- Data accuracy is being verified
- Processing is unlawful but the subject prefers restriction over erasure
- The controller no longer needs the data but the subject requires it for legal claims
- The organization is verifying a data erasure request

> **Legal recourse:** Data subjects can contact the controller directly or file a complaint with their national DPA.

## 5. Organizational Responsibilities

### 5.1 Obtain Informed Consent

GDPR reversed the default: users are now **automatically exempt** from data collection unless they explicitly **opt in**. Consent must be:
- Freely given
- Specific and informed
- Unambiguous

Controllers must disclose: what data will be collected, how it will be used, how long it will be stored, and with whom it will be shared.

### 5.2 Secure Personal Data — Privacy by Design (Article 25 & 32)

- Data collection must default to **only the data necessary** for stated purposes
- Technical and organizational measures must prevent breaches, unlawful disclosures, loss, or destruction
- Systems must be equipped to **detect cyber threats** and have clear incident response protocols
- Data must be **securely destroyed** once its purpose is fulfilled

### 5.3 Data Transfer (Chapter V)

- Subjects must consent to data sharing with third parties
- Controllers must ensure receiving entities have **satisfactory protections**
- Extra precautions required for transfers **outside the EU**
- Many controllers use **precise legal contracts** (e.g., Standard Contractual Clauses) to hold all parties accountable

> The violation of data transfer rules led to the **largest GDPR fine in history**: Meta was fined **€1.2 billion** in May 2023 for transferring EU user data to the US using mechanisms the Irish DPC had deemed inadequate.

### 5.4 Maintain Records (Article 30)

Organizations must document:
- What data was processed (including special categories)
- Why data was processed
- User demographics where relevant
- Potential third-party recipients
- Transfer mechanisms used

### 5.5 Breach Notification (Articles 33–34)

In the event of a data breach, controllers must:

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Notify the DPA** | Within **72 hours** of becoming aware |
| **Disclose** | Nature of the breach (unlawful disclosure, loss, alteration, etc.) |
| **Report** | What personal data was affected, including special categories |
| **Estimate** | Approximate number of impacted subjects |
| **Notify data subjects** | If the breach poses a significant threat to their rights |

If full information is not available within 72 hours, submit what is available and provide the rest as soon as possible.

## 6. Penalty Framework

| Tier | Maximum Fine | Applies To |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Lower tier** | €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover | Administrative and technical violations (e.g., record-keeping failures) |
| **Upper tier** | €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover | Violations of core principles, data subject rights, or cross-border transfer rules |

> The penalty amount depends on factors including: nature and gravity of the infringement, intentional or negligent character, actions taken to mitigate damage, degree of cooperation with the DPA, and any previous infringements.
